# Geisler — The Dinner Party Strategy

## One-line summary
Geisler's onboarding framework reframes the customer's first 30 days as hosting a dinner party — six discrete acts of hospitality (invitation, welcome, introductions, the main course, dessert, the thank-you note) mapped to six emails — and the metaphor forces founders to write onboarding sequences that feel human instead of transactional.

## Why this tier
Scaffolding. Geisler is an email-marketing operator (consultant for Klaviyo, ConvertKit, Mailchimp, and many others); the Dinner Party Strategy is her branded methodology distilled from years of consulting work, not a research framework. There is no controlled study showing that "Dinner Party onboarding outperforms generic onboarding" — and indeed for many product categories the framework needs significant adaptation. What makes it load-bearing as scaffolding is the metaphor itself: it forces the founder to think about onboarding as a relationship to nurture across six distinct moments rather than as a single "welcome email" or a generic drip sequence. The metaphor produces work that wouldn't otherwise happen.

## Key concepts
- **Step 1 — The Invitation.** Pre-onboarding. The signup confirmation. Job: set expectations for what the customer just walked into. This is not the welcome email; it's the email that says "we got your RSVP, here's what to expect."
- **Step 2 — The Welcome.** First touch after signup. Job: make the customer feel seen and oriented. Personal voice, no upsell, no feature dump. The job is hospitality.
- **Step 3 — The Introductions.** Introduce the cast: the founder, the team, the most important features as if they were people. Build context. The customer should know who's at the table.
- **Step 4 — The Main Course.** The first real value moment. Get the customer to the action that delivers the product's core benefit — what Reforge would call the "aha moment." The email's job is to remove friction from that single action, not to enumerate all the things the product can do.
- **Step 5 — Dessert.** A delight moment. A surprise: a hidden feature, a personal note, a power-user tip the customer wouldn't have found on their own. Builds reciprocity.
- **Step 6 — The Thank-You Note.** End-of-onboarding email. Recap what the customer accomplished, ask for one piece of feedback, transition the customer from "new" to "regular." Sets the tone for the long-term lifecycle relationship.
- **The voice discipline.** Across all six emails, the writing is plain-language, first-person, conversational, and built around the customer's name and context. The discipline rejects "Dear Customer" / "Welcome to [Product]!" generic copy as a bright-line failure mode.
- **One CTA per email.** Each email asks the customer to take exactly one action. Multiple CTAs split attention and reduce action rates.

## Direct quotes (fair use, attributed)

1. "Onboarding isn't a sequence of emails. It's a sequence of moments. If you wouldn't say it to a guest at your dinner table, don't say it in an onboarding email." — paraphrase of Geisler's recurring framing on the Dinner Party concept, [valgeisler.com](https://valgeisler.com/).

2. "The welcome email isn't the first email. The welcome email is the first email *after* you've already set expectations. Don't skip the invitation." — paraphrase of Geisler's "Invitation vs Welcome" distinction.

3. "Most onboarding emails fail because they treat the customer like a database record. The Dinner Party metaphor forces you to treat them like a person at your table." — paraphrase of Geisler's central thesis.

## When to cite
Cited by `lifecycle-lead` in `onboarding-flow.md` (the six-step structure is the framework the lead extends with project-specific timing and content) and in any voice / tone guidance for transactional / lifecycle emails. The lifecycle-lead should adapt the six steps to the project's actual user journey rather than pasting them verbatim — Step 4 (The Main Course) for elocal_clone is "complete contractor profile," not a generic SaaS aha moment.

## Anti-citation guidance
Do not cite the Dinner Party Strategy as universally optimal — for high-velocity transactional products (e.g., a one-time-use marketplace booking) the six-step structure is over-engineered, and a two-email or three-email sequence is more appropriate. Do not cite the framework as evidence of effectiveness — Geisler is explicit that the metaphor is the contribution; the *measurable impact* of using it varies by product, audience, and execution quality.
