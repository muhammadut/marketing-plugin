# Dunford — Obviously Awesome (Marketing Application Notes)

## One-line summary
Dunford's 10-step positioning process is the single most useful forcing function on this reading list — it makes the founder enumerate competitive alternatives (including "do nothing"), and that one step is where most positioning work breaks. Cross-pollinated from `brand-plugin/knowledge/strategy/dunford-obviously-awesome.md`; this version adds marketing-specific application notes for paid, SEO, and lifecycle.

## Why this tier
Scaffolding, not evidence. Dunford is an operator (she ran positioning at several B2B software companies), not an academic, and the book contains no studies. What it does contain is a process so specific that you cannot complete it without doing the work. The load-bearing step for **marketing** is the one almost every founder skips — Step 4, the competitive alternatives enumeration — because most ad copy fails not because the words are wrong but because the founder is targeting the wrong frame of reference. If your value proposition is not obviously better than $0 + an hour of inconvenience, no amount of clever ad copy will fix the underlying positioning hole.

## Key concepts (with marketing application notes)
- **Step 1 — Customers who love your product.** *Marketing application:* this is the input to the ICP doc and the seed for any audience targeting. Don't run paid traffic until Step 1 has yielded named, contactable, repeat customers you can call.
- **Step 2 — Form a positioning team.** *Marketing application:* the team must include whoever owns paid (because they will be writing the headlines and choosing the audience), not just the founder.
- **Step 3 — Drop positioning baggage.** *Marketing application:* drop the founder-story framing in ad copy. The customer doesn't care about your origin story until after the conversion.
- **Step 4 — List true competitive alternatives.** *Marketing application:* "do nothing," "use a spreadsheet," "call a friend," "post on Reddit" are competitive alternatives. Your ad copy has to be obviously better than the cheapest of these. For the elocal_clone home-services case, the dominant competitive alternative is "ask a neighbor for a referral" — and that is what your messaging is competing with, not other call-routing platforms.
- **Step 5 — Isolate unique attributes.** *Marketing application:* every headline and hero-section claim should anchor on an attribute only you have. Generic claims ("fast and affordable") fail this test; specific claims ("verified callback within 60 seconds") pass it.
- **Step 6 — Map attributes to value.** *Marketing application:* this is the bridge to ad copy. An attribute is a feature; a value is what the customer gets from the feature. Headlines should sell the value, body copy should justify with the attribute.
- **Step 7 — Best-fit segment.** *Marketing application:* the best-fit segment is the one whose pain is most acute and whose willingness to pay is highest. Targeting them through Google Ads keywords means choosing query intent that signals their pain, not generic category queries.
- **Step 8 — Market frame of reference.** *Marketing application:* the frame of reference is the headline category your ad lives in. "Find a plumber" frames you as a plumber directory; "talk to a verified plumber in 60 seconds" frames you as something different and forces a different competitive comparison.
- **Step 9 — Layer on a trend.** *Marketing application:* sparingly. Trend-anchored copy ages quickly.
- **Step 10 — Capture in a document.** *Marketing application:* the doc lives in `.marketing/positioning/positioning.md` and every other marketing artifact links back to it.

## Anti-citation guidance
Do not cite Dunford for B2C consumer positioning without qualification — the book's examples are overwhelmingly B2B software, and the "market category" step behaves differently in consumer contexts where category membership is emotional rather than procurement-driven. For elocal_clone the consumer side (homeowner with a leak) is more emotional than the contractor side (small business buying a lead), and Dunford's framework needs more adaptation on the homeowner side.
