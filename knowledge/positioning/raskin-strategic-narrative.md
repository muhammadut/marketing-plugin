# Raskin — Strategic Narrative

## One-line summary
Raskin's five-element strategic narrative — Name the Big Change, Name the Enemy, Promise the Promised Land, Show Evidence of the Obstacles, Show Evidence the New Way Works — is a forcing function for translating positioning into a story that a sales-led B2B company can use across the funnel, from pitch decks to landing pages to keynote talks.

## Why this tier
Scaffolding. Raskin is a positioning consultant; the framework is distilled from his work with companies including Salesforce, Drift, ServiceTitan, and Yammer, and the most commonly-cited case study is the Drift "we hate forms" narrative he helped build. There is no controlled study showing that Raskin-shaped narratives outperform other shapes — there are individual case studies showing material impact at specific companies, which is operator evidence at best. What makes the framework load-bearing as scaffolding is the discipline of forcing the founder to name an enemy explicitly, which most founders refuse to do because it feels confrontational. The refusal is the failure mode; the naming is the work.

## Key concepts
- **Element 1 — Name the Big Change in the World.** A shift in the world that is making the old way unviable. Not a feature claim about your product; a claim about the world. Example (Drift): "Buyers are no longer willing to fill out forms to get a sales call." The change is the world; the product is the response.
- **Element 2 — Name the Enemy.** The thing that has to die for the new way to win. Could be an old technology, an old process, an old assumption, an old vendor category. Naming an enemy explicitly is uncomfortable; the discomfort is why most founders skip it; the skipping is why most positioning is mush.
- **Element 3 — Promise the Promised Land.** The vivid, concrete future state where customers using the new way prevail. Specific enough that the prospect can picture themselves in it.
- **Element 4 — Show Evidence of the Obstacles to the Promised Land.** Why the obvious-seeming path won't work. This is the section that earns the right to talk about your specific solution by demonstrating that no easier path will get the prospect to the Promised Land.
- **Element 5 — Show Evidence the New Way Works.** Customer stories, data, demos. Proof that your specific path actually delivers the Promised Land. Comes last, after the world-frame and the obstacles have done the persuasive work.
- **Sales-led vs marketing-led use.** The framework was built for B2B sales-led companies and works best when the strategic narrative shapes both the pitch deck and the marketing site so reps and ads carry the same story. For a marketplace or consumer business, the narrative often lives more in landing pages and About pages than in sales decks.
- **The enemy is not a competitor.** Raskin is explicit that naming a specific competitor is the wrong move (legally risky, reputationally narrow, hard to evolve). The enemy is a paradigm, a behavior, a vendor category, or a default assumption — never a single named company.

1. "The most powerful pitches don't start with the product. They start with a story about how the world is changing, and an enemy that has to die for the new way to win." — paraphrase of Andy Raskin's recurring framing across his blog and Medium essays, [andyraskin.com](https://andyraskin.com/).

2. "Naming an enemy is uncomfortable. That's why most companies don't. That's also why most positioning is mush." — paraphrase of Raskin's recurring framing on Element 2.

3. "Don't pitch the product. Pitch the new world your product makes possible. Then, and only then, show evidence the new world works." — paraphrase of Raskin's narrative-sequencing argument.

## When to cite
Cited by `positioning-lead` in `messaging-hierarchy.md` (the strategic narrative is the umbrella story under which the messaging hierarchy nests) and in any landing-page brief that asks for a hero-section narrative. Cited by `marketing-director` when reconciling positioning across sub-verticals — if every sub-vertical is telling a different story, the strategic narrative is what re-aligns them.

## Anti-citation guidance
Do not cite Raskin as authority for B2C consumer positioning — the framework was built for sales-led B2B and the enemy / obstacles / proof structure is heavier than most consumer purchases warrant. Do not cite Raskin as evidence the framework outperforms alternatives — there is no controlled comparison; the case studies are post-hoc. Do not name a single competitor as the enemy — Raskin is explicit that this is the wrong move and the failure mode is litigation risk and pivot brittleness.
