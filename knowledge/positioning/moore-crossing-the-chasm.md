# Moore — Crossing the Chasm

## One-line summary
Moore's chasm model adapts Rogers' Technology Adoption Lifecycle (innovators → early adopters → early majority → late majority → laggards) and identifies the gap between early adopters and the early majority as the failure point where most disruptive products die — and prescribes a beachhead strategy of dominating one tightly-defined segment before expanding.

## Why this tier
Scaffolding. Moore is a consultant and the book is operator framework, not research — the underlying Diffusion of Innovations curve from Rogers (1962) is empirically grounded sociology, but the specific "chasm" Moore identifies between early adopters and the early majority is Moore's interpretive overlay, supported by case studies rather than controlled measurement. What makes the framework load-bearing as scaffolding is the **beachhead discipline**: the framework forces the founder to commit to dominating one narrow segment before going broad, which is the work most founders skip in favor of "we'll target everyone who could possibly want it." The skipping is the failure mode; the commitment is the work.

## Key concepts
- **The Technology Adoption Lifecycle.** Borrowed from Rogers (1962) and re-purposed for tech: innovators (2.5%), early adopters (13.5%), early majority (34%), late majority (34%), laggards (16%). Each segment has different motivations, different proof requirements, and different willingness to tolerate roughness in the product.
- **The chasm.** The gap between early adopters (who are willing to be unreasonable in their pursuit of competitive advantage) and the early majority (who are pragmatists waiting for proof from people like themselves). Most products that fail to scale fail here — they have a passionate cohort of early adopters but cannot get reference accounts the early majority will trust.
- **The beachhead segment.** The specific, narrow customer group you commit to dominating first. The criteria: a single, unified buyer persona; a single compelling reason to buy; a small enough market that you can become the obvious leader; a market with adjacent segments you can expand into once you've won the beachhead.
- **The whole product.** The early majority does not buy a feature — they buy a complete solution including documentation, partner ecosystem, training, support, integrations, and case studies. The "whole product" is everything around the core offering that makes it adoptable by a non-adventurous buyer. Building the whole product is the work that crosses the chasm.
- **References from people like me.** The early majority does not trust early-adopter testimonials — they trust testimonials from people who look like them. Reference accounts from the beachhead segment are the asset that unlocks the next segment.
- **Bowling pin strategy.** After winning the beachhead, expand into adjacent segments where you can use the beachhead reference accounts as proof. Each segment knocks down the next one. The expansion sequence is part of the strategy, not an afterthought.
- **The tornado.** Moore's later term (in *Inside the Tornado*) for the rapid mass-market adoption phase that follows successfully crossing the chasm. The behavior that wins in the tornado phase is different from what won the beachhead — operational excellence, channel scale, distribution dominance.

## Direct quotes (fair use, attributed)

1. "The chasm represents the gulf between two distinct marketplaces for technology products — the first, an early market dominated by early adopters and insiders who are quick to appreciate the nature and benefits of the new development, and the second, a mainstream market representing 'the rest of us,' people who want the benefits of new technology but who do not want to 'experience' it in all its gory details." — Geoffrey A. Moore, *Crossing the Chasm*, 3rd edition, Introduction.

2. "Target a single beachhead. Dominate it. Use it as the launching pad for adjacent segments. Do not try to be everything to everyone in the early years; you cannot afford it and you will not survive it." — paraphrase of Moore's beachhead framing across multiple chapters.

3. "The early majority does not buy from the early adopters' references. They buy from references that look like themselves. Build the whole product for the beachhead and earn those references before you try to expand." — paraphrase of Moore's whole-product / reference-accounts argument.

## When to cite
Cited by `positioning-lead` in `positioning.md` (the beachhead segment is the input to the ICP definition) and in any "expansion plan" output that maps how the beachhead segment leads to adjacent segments. Cited by `marketing-director` when sub-vertical leads are proposing horizontal targeting that spreads the beachhead resources too thin.

## Anti-citation guidance
Do not cite Moore as a recommendation to ignore non-beachhead customers entirely — the framework is about prioritization of marketing and sales effort, not about refusing service to anyone who walks in. Do not cite the "chasm" as a single fixed-position gap — Moore himself has revised the model multiple times, and the gap behaves differently for SaaS, marketplaces, and consumer products than for the enterprise hardware/software cases the book uses as examples.
