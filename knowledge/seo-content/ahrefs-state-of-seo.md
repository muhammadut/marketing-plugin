# Ahrefs — State of SEO and Backlinks Studies

## One-line summary
Ahrefs publishes the largest open backlink-graph studies in the SEO industry; their annual research output is the closest thing to "what the SEO web actually looks like" that exists in public — sourced from a backlink index that has expanded from 3.3 trillion links in 2022 to roughly 35 trillion by 2025.

## Why this tier
Evidence. Ahrefs runs the second- or first-largest backlink crawler in the industry (depending on the year), and their published findings are based on actual indexed link data at trillion-link scale, not opinion polls. The tier is "evidence" specifically for the **graph-shaped** observations (link decay rates, follow vs nofollow ratios, reciprocal-link prevalence, the rate at which top-ranking pages acquire new links) and for the **competitive benchmarking** the Ahrefs index enables. The tier is *scaffolding* for the strategy posts they layer on top of the data — those are operator interpretations, not findings. Decay note: Ahrefs republishes the headline statistics roughly once per year; the seo-content-researcher should refresh against the current `ahrefs.com/blog/seo-statistics/` post.

## Key concepts
- **Backlink index growth.** From 3.3 trillion external backlinks (2022) to ~35 trillion (2025), a roughly 10x expansion in indexed link surface. Internal backlinks indexed: ~27.2 trillion as of March 2025 (a ~112% increase since 2017). The takeaway is not "the web is bigger" — it is that Ahrefs' detection threshold is more sensitive, so their per-page data is more complete.
- **Link decay.** Roughly 66.5% of links indexed in the last nine years are now dead (404, redirected, or de-published). The strategic implication: a backlink portfolio is not a one-time asset; absent active maintenance, the half-life of a "permanent" link is shorter than founders assume. Plan for active link reclamation.
- **Reciprocal links are surprisingly common at the top.** ~43.7% of top-ranking pages have at least some reciprocal links. The takeaway is not "go build link exchanges" — Google still penalizes link schemes — but rather that a small fraction of organic reciprocal links is normal in a healthy profile and not a red flag.
- **New-link velocity for top-ranking pages.** Most top-ranking pages get followed backlinks from new websites at a rate of 5-14.5% per month. The strategic implication: ranking pages compound — they earn links because they rank — and a page that has plateaued is at risk of being passed by a competitor that is still acquiring links.
- **Follow / nofollow / UGC / sponsored ratio.** Approximately 10.6% of all backlinks to the top 110,000 sites are nofollow, 0.44% use `rel=ugc`, and 0.01% use `rel=sponsored`. Healthy profiles are mostly follow links from non-UGC, non-sponsored sources; profiles with abnormally high nofollow ratios suggest reliance on forum / comment / directory links.
- **Link-building tactic survey.** In Ahrefs' 2025 link-building practitioner survey, digital PR was ranked the most effective single tactic for the year by 48.6% of respondents, ahead of guest posting, broken-link building, and outreach.

## Anti-citation guidance
Do not cite Ahrefs statistics as if they prove **causal** SEO claims (e.g., "more links cause higher rankings"). Ahrefs data is correlational and cross-sectional; it tells you what the top of the SERP looks like, not what made it the top of the SERP. Do not cite Ahrefs research as Google's position — Ahrefs is a third-party crawler making inferences, not a signal Google publishes. Refresh annually; old Ahrefs statistics get cited as if they were current well past their useful life.
