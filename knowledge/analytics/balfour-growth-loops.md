# Balfour — Growth Loops and 4 Fits

## One-line summary
Balfour replaces the linear "growth funnel" mental model with the more accurate **growth loop** — a closed-loop system where outputs from one cycle become inputs to the next — and the 4 Fits framework (product/market, market/channel, channel/model, model/product) as a forcing function for diagnosing why growth has stalled.

## Why this tier
Scaffolding. Balfour is an operator (ex-HubSpot VP Growth, founder of Reforge) and the framework is operator pattern-matching from running growth at scale and teaching the Reforge growth program to thousands of practitioners. There are no controlled studies showing that "loop thinking outperforms funnel thinking" — there are case studies of companies that grew successfully using loop frameworks. What makes the framework load-bearing as scaffolding is the diagnostic power: the 4 Fits framework forces a founder facing stalled growth to ask "is the issue product-market, market-channel, channel-model, or model-product?" rather than reflexively reaching for "we need more traffic." The reframe usually identifies the actual constraint, which is usually not the one the team has been working on.

## Key concepts
- **Growth loops, not growth funnels.** A funnel is a linear sequence (visit → signup → activate → retain → refer) where you measure conversion at each step. A loop is closed (each cohort of new users produces inputs — content, links, referrals, data, money — that drive the next cohort of new users). The funnel framing makes growth feel like an optimization problem; the loop framing makes growth feel like a compounding system. Compounding systems beat optimization in the long run.
- **The four canonical loop types.** (1) Content loops (users create content that ranks in search and brings new users). (2) Viral loops (users invite other users). (3) Paid loops (paid ads bring users whose LTV pays for more ads). (4) Sales loops (sales-led B2B where existing customers become reference accounts that close new customers). Most companies have one dominant loop; trying to run all four simultaneously is a common failure mode.
- **The 4 Fits framework.**
  - *Product-Market Fit.* The product solves a real problem for a real audience.
  - *Market-Channel Fit.* The audience can be reached through a specific scalable channel.
  - *Channel-Model Fit.* The channel produces unit economics consistent with the business model (e.g., paid search at $7.85 CPC needs a margin structure that supports the implied CAC).
  - *Model-Product Fit.* The business model (subscription, transaction, marketplace take rate) is consistent with the product's natural usage frequency and willingness to pay.
- **Where stalls actually live.** Most "growth has slowed" diagnoses jump to "our channel isn't working" when the actual constraint is one of the other three fits. The 4 Fits forces the diagnostic to scan all four before concluding which is broken.
- **The North Star Metric.** A single metric that captures the value the product delivers, lagged enough to reflect retention but sensitive enough to move with weekly product work. Typically a usage metric ("monthly active rooms hosted" for Airbnb) rather than revenue, because revenue is too lagging and too multifactorial.
- **Bowtie funnel for marketplaces.** Marketplaces have two-sided funnels (supply and demand) plus a transaction loop in the middle. The two sides have to be balanced — over-investing in demand when supply is the constraint wastes the investment.

## Anti-citation guidance
Do not cite Balfour as evidence loops *outperform* funnels in measured terms — there's no controlled comparison; the framing is a useful mental model, not a measured uplift. Do not assume the 4 Fits map cleanly to a single product launch — the framework is most useful for diagnosing stalls in mature products, less useful for planning a zero-to-one launch where product-market fit is still the open question.
